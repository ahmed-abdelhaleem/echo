# 01 — Product Vision

## The one-sentence pitch

**Echo is a game that plays you back.** You live one day in a stranger's life, and at the end Echo shows you who *you* really are — in art, not in labels.

---

## The problem

People in their teens and twenties are more curious about themselves than any generation before them — but the tools available to satisfy that curiosity are split into two camps, and both are broken:

1. **Personality quizzes** (16Personalities, Buzzfeed-style, TikTok tests) — fun, viral, but scientifically meaningless. People know they're being asked questions and answer the way they want to be seen.
2. **Validated assessments** (Myers-Briggs, Hogan, Big Five questionnaires) — accurate-ish, but clinical, boring, expensive, and trapped inside HR departments and clinical settings.

Neither feels like *play*. Neither produces something a 19-year-old would screenshot and send to a friend at 1 a.m.

There is a third category that doesn't really exist yet: an experience that is **actually fun**, **scientifically grounded**, and **shareable**. That is the space Echo is built to occupy.

---

## The core insight

> *People reveal themselves most honestly when they think they're playing a character.*

Self-report assessments fail because of social desirability bias — people answer as the person they want to be. Echo bypasses this entirely by never asking the player about themselves. The player is told they are making decisions *for a fictional character*. They drop the social mask. And then every micro-decision they make — which message to open first, whether to lie to spare a feeling, whether to spend a free hour alone or with people — is real data about who they actually are.

This isn't a trick on the user. It's disclosed in the privacy summary and explained in the result. But it is what makes the result *feel true* in a way no quiz ever has.

---

## The experience

A single playthrough is roughly **20–30 minutes**. During that time the player lives one fictional day in the life of a stranger — a different stranger each season, written with care.

- Morning, midday, afternoon, evening — four acts.
- 15–25 micro-decisions per playthrough.
- No timers, no scores, no "right answers," no XP, no leaderboards.
- Atmospheric soundtrack, minimal but evocative art direction.
- Choices presented as natural-language options people would actually say or do — not as labeled archetypes.

At the end, instead of a label, Echo generates:

1. **A Portrait** — a unique piece of generative visual art that represents how the player moved through the day. Color palette, density, line quality, and motif are all driven by trait values.
2. **A short prose reflection** — three to five sentences in second person, never clinical, never flattering, always specific. ("You notice exits before you notice people. You give second chances quietly, without announcing them.")
3. **An optional deeper view** — for players who want it, the underlying trait map with plain-language explanations.

The Portrait is what gets shared. It is the viral asset. It is also unique enough to function as a kind of soft identity — a sigil.

---

## What Echo is *not*

To stay honest about scope, here are deliberate non-goals:

- **Not a competitive game.** No leaderboards, no rankings, no "high score" personality.
- **Not a diagnosis.** Echo describes patterns, not pathologies. It will never tell anyone they have a disorder. (See `08 — Data, Privacy & Compliance`.)
- **Not a dating app.** Compatibility features may eventually exist but are not the wedge.
- **Not a chatbot or AI companion.** Echo speaks to you four times — at the start, in the middle, and twice at the end. That's it. Restraint is part of the brand.
- **Not infinite content.** Each season is a curated, finite story. Quality over quantity.

---

## Brand soul

Three words: **quiet, specific, generous.**

- **Quiet** — no notifications screaming for attention, no streaks, no FOMO mechanics. Echo respects that what it's doing is intimate.
- **Specific** — never says "you are an introvert." Always says something true that only this player would recognize about themselves.
- **Generous** — never roasts the player. The framing is always *this is who you are, and that is worth seeing clearly.* Self-knowledge as a gift, not a verdict.

The closest brand reference points: **Headspace** (calm, considered), **Letterboxd** (specific, literary), **Monument Valley** (atmospheric, restrained), **The School of Life** (thoughtful self-inquiry as product).

The brand reference points to avoid: **Duolingo** (gamified pressure), **BetterHelp** (clinical, ads-heavy), **16Personalities** (label-driven, shallow).

---

## Who Echo is for

### Primary audience — Players (consumer)
- Ages 15–28
- Self-curious — already takes quizzes, reads horoscopes, has opinions about their MBTI even if they say they don't
- Strong on iOS in EU, NA, and increasingly LATAM and SEA
- Will share something on Instagram or TikTok if it looks beautiful and feels true

### Secondary audience — Institutions (B2B)
- School counselors and student wellbeing programs (ages 13–18 with appropriate safeguards)
- University career and coaching services
- Adolescent and young-adult therapists (as a conversation-starter tool, not a diagnostic)
- Youth coaching and life-coaching practices

The institutional buyer pays per seat and gets a clean dashboard. The institution never sees raw answers — only aggregate or, with explicit player consent, the Portrait itself. This is a feature, not a limitation.

---

## What success looks like — three horizons

**Year 1.** 100,000 consumer players in EU, 50%+ completion rate, NPS > 50. First three B2B pilot contracts signed (schools or coaching practices). Seed round closed.

**Year 2.** 1M+ consumer players globally. €1M+ ARR across consumer + B2B. Echo is referenced unprompted by users on TikTok and Reddit. Series A conversations begin.

**Year 3.** Echo is the default answer when an 18-year-old asks "what's a good personality test that doesn't suck?" Series A closed. B2B becomes the larger revenue line. Portraits are a recognizable visual format people screenshot and share without explanation.
