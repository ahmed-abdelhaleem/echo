# 12 — Investor Pitch Outline

> This is a structural outline for the investor narrative, not a finished deck. Use it as the spine for a pre-seed deck (~12 slides), a seed deck (~15 slides), and the conversation itself. The same arc works at all three stages with different proof points.

## The narrative arc — what every investor conversation should leave them with

In one minute:

> *People in their teens and twenties are more curious about themselves than any generation in history, and the tools they have are broken. The fun ones — TikTok quizzes, MBTI — aren't real. The real ones — Hogan, Gallup — aren't fun and aren't for them. A small Japanese indie game called Refind Self quietly sold half a million copies at $4 each in two years by getting one thing right: it played you back to yourself. We're building the platform version of that. A game across iPhone, Android, Mac, and Windows in which players live one day in a stranger's life, and our engine maps every micro-decision they make to validated psychological dimensions. They get back a beautiful, shareable artifact that feels like seeing themselves clearly for the first time. Consumer subscription pays for it; schools, coaching practices, and youth wellbeing programs license it per seat. The dataset of millions of micro-decisions mapped to validated personality structure is the long-term moat. Refind Self proved the demand. Nobody has built the platform yet. We're the team to do it.*

If they're still listening after that, you have a conversation.

## Pre-seed deck — 12 slides

### Slide 1 — Title
- Product name: Echo
- Tagline: *A game that plays you back.*
- Founder name, contact, date.
- One image: a sample Portrait. The whole pitch should be visually anchored to what the Portrait looks like.

### Slide 2 — The problem
- One stat: estimated tens of millions of Gen Z take personality tests every year.
- One emotional line: the existing tools are either fun and meaningless, or accurate and boring.
- One quote (from real qualitative research in M0): a 19-year-old describing why MBTI "isn't really me."

### Slide 3 — The insight
- People reveal themselves most honestly when they're playing a character.
- This is why Refind Self works.
- This is what every existing personality test gets wrong.

### Slide 4 — What Echo is
- 20-minute narrative game across all four platforms.
- One day in a stranger's life. 15–25 vignettes.
- Output: a parametric Portrait + short prose reflection.
- The Portrait is what gets shared.

### Slide 5 — The proof in the market
- Refind Self: 500k copies sold, $4 each, solo dev, no marketing.
- 16Personalities: 100M+ tests, weak retention, no platform.
- Hogan/Gallup: $300+ per assessment, B2B only.
- Echo's position: fun + scientific + social + B2B-ready + youth-safe. No one else holds all five.

### Slide 6 — The product (with screens)
- Two or three screens: a vignette, a Portrait, a friend comparison.
- This is where the deck has to be visually distinctive. Echo's brand is the differentiation; the deck must reflect it.

### Slide 7 — The business model
- Echo+ consumer subscription at €4.99/mo or €39.99/yr.
- Echo for Institutions at €3–25/seat/mo by segment.
- Two revenue streams that compound: consumer drives reach, B2B drives revenue density.

### Slide 8 — Year-3 model snapshot
- 2–4M monthly active players.
- 80k–160k Echo+ subscribers.
- 80–150 institutional customers.
- €7M–€14M ARR target.
- Gross margin 75–85%.

### Slide 9 — The moat
- Brand and aesthetic specificity.
- Sustained content cadence — every Season compounds.
- Validated trait engine — citable science.
- Dataset of (consented, anonymized) micro-decisions mapped to validated personality — increasingly hard to replicate.
- B2B distribution.

### Slide 10 — Team
- Founder: backend/architecture, content-iteration discipline.
- Advisor circle: mobile, DevOps, ML, backend.
- Honest gap: distribution and go-to-market. Asking for an investor who can help here.

### Slide 11 — The ask
- Raising €250k–€500k pre-seed.
- Use of funds: two engineering hires (Flutter + Python/ML), Season 2 production, V1 cross-platform launch, EU+UK marketing test, DPO and compliance setup.
- 18-month runway to V1 launch and seed-readiness.

### Slide 12 — Why now
- LLM and generative-art tooling makes personalized output economic for the first time.
- Refind Self proved demand.
- Cross-platform engines (Flutter) make one team viable on four platforms.
- Gen Z is exhausted by quizzes that don't feel true.
- Regulatory and institutional appetite for ethical youth wellbeing tools is at an all-time high.

## Questions investors will ask, and the honest answer to each

These are the questions to be ready for. Drafted answers, not scripts — say it in your voice.

### "What's the moat? AI image gen is commodity now."

Echo's moat is not the technology. The technology is replaceable. The moat is the **content cadence, the brand voice, the validated trait engine, the institutional distribution, and the (eventually) proprietary dataset**. Generating a Portrait is easy. Generating a Portrait that a 19-year-old in Berlin screenshots and sends to her best friend at 1 a.m. requires hundreds of hours of writing, dozens of design iterations, and trait weights that took a research-grade process to calibrate. That work compounds; it cannot be cloned overnight.

### "Why won't Apple or Google just ship this?"

They might. The mitigation is not to out-resource them — we can't. The mitigation is to be unmistakably ourselves, where their default is generic; to own institutional distribution, which is structurally hard for them; and to have a year or more of content compounding before they can plausibly enter. The closest comparable, Refind Self, sold half a million copies under the noses of Apple and Google for two years.

### "Why subscription? Refind Self did fine as a one-time purchase."

Refind Self did fine; it also stopped at $2M lifetime gross for a solo dev. We are not building Refind Self at the same scale; we are building the platform version that ships new Seasons, supports friend comparison, integrates with schools, and improves continuously. The work supporting that costs more than a one-time purchase covers. Subscription also unlocks B2B; you can't sell seats to a school against a one-time consumer purchase.

### "What about a free, ad-supported tier?"

Explicitly out of scope. Echo is built on trust and on user-experience quality. Ads — especially targeted ads near personality inference data — would destroy the brand and create compliance exposure with minors that no amount of revenue would compensate. This is a "no, ever" answer, not a "maybe later" answer.

### "Why EU first instead of US?"

The regulatory complexity of operating with adolescents in the US, especially the state-by-state patchwork (CCPA, BIPA, FERPA), means starting US-first would slow us down. Starting EU-first with GDPR-K-grade compliance as the baseline lets us expand to the US later with a stronger posture, not a weaker one.

### "What's the path to a venture-scale outcome?"

Three paths, in increasing scale:
1. **The boring path:** Echo becomes a profitable €20–30M ARR business serving consumer + B2B in EU/UK/NA. Acquired by an education or wellness platform at a healthy multiple.
2. **The platform path:** Echo's trait engine becomes a primitive that other products license. Echo itself becomes one of several surfaces. €100M+ ARR plausible.
3. **The category path:** Echo becomes the default answer to "how should we understand who we are?" — the way Letterboxd became the default for film and Headspace became the default for meditation. €500M+ enterprise value possible.

We are building for path 2, knowing that path 1 is a fine outcome and path 3 is the upside.

### "What does failure look like, and what's the soft landing?"

The honest failure mode is that the product doesn't feel true to enough players, and the consumer flywheel doesn't engage. In that scenario, the institutional B2B path is still viable — Echo becomes a useful B2B-only tool serving schools and coaches with a smaller consumer top of funnel, and the company is a quieter business at €3–5M ARR. The capital deployed and timeline mean this is not an existential outcome; it is an underperforming one. We do not start the company to optimize for soft landings, but the company is not bet on a single outcome.

### "Why this team?"

The founder's strength is exactly the strength this company needs: sustained iteration on quality. The work that compounds into Echo's moat — vignette writing, trait calibration, brand voice — is months and years of patient improvement, not a single brilliant insight. The founder has the temperament for that, plus the technical depth to architect the system and the humility to hire around the gaps. The advisor circle covers the technical surface area. The first external hires are scoped to the immediate gaps. The honest gap is distribution and fundraising, which is exactly what an investor partner brings.

## What to bring to the meeting beyond the deck

- **A playable demo on your phone.** Even a single vignette + a Portrait is more persuasive than ten slides.
- **A live Portrait of the investor.** If the conversation goes well and the trust is there, offer to let them play. Hand them your phone for 20 minutes if they'll take it. The product is the pitch.
- **The competitive comparison matrix from `02_Market_Analysis.md`** — investors will want to know exactly why this isn't Refind Self with subscription pricing. The matrix answers it.
- **The privacy posture summary from `08_Data_Privacy_Compliance.md`** — investors with any experience in consumer-facing products with minors will ask. Having a clear answer differentiates you from the founders who treat privacy as a chore.

## What *not* to do in investor meetings

- Don't promise that Echo will be the next Calm. Comparisons reduce; they don't elevate. Echo's pitch is that it's a new thing.
- Don't over-engineer the projection numbers. Year-3 ARR ranges are healthier than precise single-point forecasts.
- Don't avoid the Big Tech question. Address it directly; the investor has already thought of it.
- Don't pitch the AI angle as the differentiator. The AI is table stakes; the differentiator is brand, content, and distribution. Leading with AI signals weakness.
- Don't apologize for the EU-first strategy. Explain it once, clearly, and move on. Founders who pre-apologize for their plan invite scrutiny they didn't need to invite.
